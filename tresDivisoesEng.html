Context of the problem

The two friends meet a rich sheikh named Salem Nasair, who was starving in the desert. who managed to escape an invasion of his caravan by a host of Persian nomads from the desert. Then, the Man Who Calculated brought 5 loaves of bread, where the sheikh promised to pay with 8 gold coins for the bread he ate when they arrived in Baghdad.

This was done, however, the sheikh said that Beremiz would receive 5 coins for the 5 loaves and Bagdali, 3 coins for the 3 loaves. The Man who calculated politely corrected Nasair, saying that the division was not mathematically correct, explaining that if he gave 5 loaves, he should receive 7 coins and his companion one for the 3 loaves.
Vizier Ibrahim, keenly interested in the case, asked for justification on how to pay for the bread. Then Beremiz approaches the prestigious minister and explains the solution.

Resolution[

"When, during the journey, we were hungry, I would take a loaf of bread from the box in which they were stored and divide it into three pieces, each of us eating one of those pieces. If I gave 5 loaves, I gave, of course, 15 pieces; if my companion gave 3 loaves, he contributed 9 pieces. There were, therefore, a total of 24 pieces, therefore, 8 pieces for each one. 7; my companion gave, as I said, 9 pieces and also ate 8; therefore, he only gave 1. The 7 pieces that I gave and that the Bagdali provided formed the 8 that fit Sheikh Salem Nasair. May I receive 7 coins and my companion only one."

The Grand Vizier, after paying the highest praise to the Man Who Calculated, ordered that the coins be given to him in a fair manner. The demonstration presented by the mathematician was logical, perfect and unanswerable.
The Man Who Calculated left everyone amazed with his shrewdness and enviable talent. His calculating ability was exceptional.
